Srinagar - The Summer Capital

Srinagar is the largest city and the summer capital of Jammu and Kashmir. Situated in the Kashmir Valley along the banks of the Jhelum River, it is famous for its stunning natural beauty, serene lakes, and splendid Mughal gardens.

The city's history dates back to the 3rd century BC when it was founded by Emperor Ashoka. Throughout its history, Srinagar has been an important center of Buddhism and later became a prominent city under Mughal rule, which is evident in its architectural heritage.

Today, Srinagar serves as the cultural and economic hub of the Kashmir Valley. The city experiences a humid subtropical climate with distinct seasons, making it a year-round destination, though the best time to visit is from March to October when the weather is pleasant and the gardens are in full bloom.

Highlights
  • Dal Lake with its iconic houseboats and shikaras
  • Mughal Gardens including Shalimar Bagh, Nishat Bagh, and Chashme Shahi
  • Historic Jamia Masjid built in the 14th century
  • Shankaracharya Temple offering panoramic city views
  • Traditional Kashmiri handicrafts and cuisine
Activities
  • Shikara rides on Dal Lake and Nigeen Lake
  • Staying in traditional houseboats
  • Exploring Mughal gardens and their terraced layouts
  • Shopping for Pashmina shawls, carpets, and papier-mâché items
  • Visiting local markets and trying authentic Kashmiri food
Travel Tips
  • Book houseboats in advance during peak season (May-September)
  • Carry warm clothes even in summer as evenings can be cool
  • Respect local customs and dress modestly
  • Bargain while shopping in local markets
  • Try traditional Kashmiri dishes like Rogan Josh and Kahwa tea
Gulmarg - The Meadow of Flowers

Gulmarg, meaning 'Meadow of Flowers', is a stunning hill station located in the Baramulla district of Jammu and Kashmir. Situated at an altitude of 2,650 meters, it is one of the most popular tourist destinations in India, famous for its scenic beauty and adventure sports.

Originally called 'Gaurimarg' by shepherds, the place was renamed Gulmarg by Sultan Yusuf Shah of the Chak Dynasty in the 16th century. During British rule, it became a favorite summer retreat, and today it has developed into Asia's premier ski destination.

Gulmarg boasts the world's second-highest operating cable car, the Gulmarg Gondola, which takes visitors to Kongdoori and then to the Apharwat Peak at 4,200 meters. The destination offers breathtaking views of snow-capped peaks, including Nanga Parbat, and is surrounded by dense forests of pine and fir.

Highlights
  • Gulmarg Gondola - one of the highest cable cars in the world
  • St. Mary's Church - a historic Anglican church built in 1902
  • Gulmarg Golf Course - one of the highest green golf courses in the world
  • Alpather Lake - a high-altitude frozen lake for most of the year
  • Seven Springs - natural springs with medicinal properties
Activities
  • Skiing and snowboarding (December to March)
  • Gondola rides to Apharwat Peak
  • Trekking to Khilanmarg and Alpather Lake
  • Golfing at the high-altitude golf course
  • Pony rides through meadows and forests
Travel Tips
  • Winter visits require proper winter clothing and gear
  • Book accommodations well in advance during peak season
  • Acclimatize properly to avoid altitude sickness
  • Hire certified guides for trekking and adventure activities
  • Check weather conditions before planning outdoor activities
Pahalgam - The Valley of Shepherds

Pahalgam, which means 'Village of Shepherds', is a beautiful hill station in the Anantnag district of Jammu and Kashmir. Located on the banks of the Lidder River at an altitude of 2,740 meters, it serves as the base camp for the annual Amarnath Yatra pilgrimage.

The town was originally a small shepherd's village before developing into a popular tourist destination. Its stunning landscapes, consisting of lush green meadows, pine forests, and snow-capped mountains, have made it a favorite location for Bollywood filmmakers.

Pahalgam offers a perfect blend of tranquility and adventure, with opportunities for trekking, fishing, and river rafting. The town experiences a pleasant climate throughout the year, with summers being mild and winters bringing heavy snowfall that transforms it into a winter wonderland.

Highlights
  • Betaab Valley - named after the Bollywood movie shot here
  • Aru Valley - base for treks to Kolahoi Glacier
  • Lidder River - perfect for trout fishing and river rafting
  • Baisaran Meadows - known as 'Mini Switzerland'
  • Mamleshwar Temple - ancient Shiva temple in Pahalgam
Activities
  • Trekking to Kolahoi Glacier and Tarsar-Marsar Lakes
  • Trout fishing in Lidder River (with proper permits)
  • River rafting in the Lidder River
  • Pony rides to nearby valleys and meadows
  • Photography amidst stunning landscapes
Travel Tips
  • Carry warm clothes as temperatures can drop significantly
  • Book accommodations in advance during Amarnath Yatra season
  • Hire local guides for trekking to ensure safety
  • Respect the environment and avoid littering
  • Check road conditions before traveling during winter months
Sonamarg - The Meadow of Gold

Sonamarg, which translates to 'Meadow of Gold', is a picturesque hill station in the Ganderbal district of Jammu and Kashmir. Situated at an altitude of 2,800 meters, it serves as the gateway to Ladakh and is the last major stop on the historic Silk Road before reaching the Zoji La Pass.

The valley gets its name from the golden color of the meadow flowers that bloom during spring and summer. Surrounded by majestic glaciers and snow-capped peaks, Sonamarg is the starting point for the famous Amarnath Yatra and offers some of the most breathtaking views in the Kashmir Valley.

Sonamarg remains covered in snow during winter, transforming into a pristine white landscape, while summers reveal lush green meadows dotted with colorful flowers. The Sindh River flows through the valley, adding to its scenic charm and providing opportunities for fishing and riverside picnics.

Highlights
  • Thajiwas Glacier - a stunning glacier accessible by pony ride
  • Zoji La Pass - a high mountain pass connecting Kashmir to Ladakh
  • Krishansar and Vishansar Lakes - pristine alpine lakes
  • Baltal Valley - base camp for Amarnath Yatra
  • Sindh River - perfect for fishing and riverside activities
Activities
  • Trekking to Thajiwas Glacier and alpine lakes
  • Pony rides through the meadows and to nearby attractions
  • Trout fishing in the Sindh River
  • Photography of stunning landscapes and glaciers
  • Camping amidst natural surroundings
Travel Tips
  • Visit between May and September for the best weather conditions
  • Carry warm clothing as temperatures can be cold even in summer
  • Acclimatize properly due to the high altitude
  • Hire local guides for trekking to glaciers and remote areas
  • Check road conditions, especially if planning to cross Zoji La Pass
Leh - The Land of High Passes

Leh is the joint capital and largest town of the Union Territory of Ladakh. Situated at an altitude of 3,500 meters in the Indus River valley, it is one of the highest permanently inhabited towns in the world and serves as the administrative center of Ladakh.

The town has a rich history as an important stop on the Silk Road, with its strategic location facilitating trade between Tibet, Kashmir, and Central Asia. Leh Palace, built in the 17th century, stands as a reminder of the region's historical significance as the capital of the Himalayan kingdom of Ladakh.

Leh is characterized by its stark, high-altitude desert landscape, surrounded by rugged mountains. The town is home to several Buddhist monasteries (gompas) and offers a unique blend of Tibetan and Indian cultures. Despite the harsh climate, Leh has developed into a popular tourist destination, attracting adventure enthusiasts and cultural travelers alike.

Highlights
  • Leh Palace - a nine-story palace resembling Potala Palace in Lhasa
  • Shanti Stupa - a white-domed Buddhist stupa offering panoramic views
  • Thiksey Monastery - one of the most beautiful monasteries in Ladakh
  • Hemis Monastery - the largest and wealthiest monastery in Ladakh
  • Magnetic Hill - a gravity hill that appears to pull vehicles uphill
Activities
  • Exploring ancient Buddhist monasteries and their rich heritage
  • Trekking in the surrounding mountains and valleys
  • Mountain biking on challenging high-altitude trails
  • River rafting in the Indus and Zanskar rivers
  • Visiting local markets for traditional Ladakhi handicrafts
Travel Tips
  • Spend at least 2 days acclimatizing to the high altitude upon arrival
  • Carry sunscreen and lip balm to protect against strong UV rays
  • Stay hydrated to combat the effects of high altitude and dry climate
  • Respect local customs, especially when visiting monasteries
  • Travel between May and September when roads are accessible
Dal Lake - The Jewel of Srinagar

Dal Lake is an urban lake in Srinagar, often called the "Jewel in the crown of Kashmir" or "Srinagar's Jewel". It is the second largest lake in Jammu and Kashmir and is integral to tourism and recreation in the region. The lake is famous for its picturesque houseboats and colorful shikaras.

The lake is divided into four basins: Gagribal, Lokut Dal, Bod Dal, and Nagin. Each has its own character and charm. The Mughal gardens around the lake, built by Emperor Jahangir, add to its beauty. The lake also includes floating gardens, which are remarkable examples of hydroponic cultivation.

Dal Lake has been a source of inspiration for poets, artists, and filmmakers throughout history. Its changing moods through the seasons, from the vibrant bloom of lotus flowers in summer to the serene snow-covered landscape in winter, make it a perennial favorite among visitors.

Highlights
  • Houseboats - traditional wooden boats serving as floating hotels
  • Shikaras - colorful wooden boats used for transportation and tours
  • Floating gardens - unique agricultural practice on the lake
  • Char Chinar - four majestic Chinar trees on an island in the lake
  • Mughal Gardens - terraced gardens with fountains and pavilions
Activities
  • Shikara rides to explore different parts of the lake
  • Staying in traditional houseboats for an authentic experience
  • Visiting floating vegetable markets in the early morning
  • Photography of sunrise and sunset over the lake
  • Shopping for handicrafts from floating vendors
Travel Tips
  • Bargain for shikara rides before starting the journey
  • Book houseboats in advance during peak tourist season
  • Respect the privacy of locals living on the lake
  • Try local snacks and tea from floating vendors
  • Carry a camera to capture the stunning scenery
Betaab Valley - A Bollywood Paradise

Betaab Valley is a stunningly beautiful valley located about 15 kilometers from Pahalgam in the Anantnag district. The valley got its name after the Bollywood movie 'Betaab' was shot here in 1983. Before that, it was known as Hagan Valley or Bagan Valley.

Situated at the foothills of the Himalayas, the valley is surrounded by snow-capped mountains and lush green meadows. The crystal-clear waters of the Lidder River flow through the valley, adding to its scenic charm. The valley remains covered in snow during winter and transforms into a vibrant green paradise during summer.

Betaab Valley offers breathtaking views of the surrounding mountains and is a popular spot for picnics, photography, and nature walks. The valley's pristine beauty and tranquil atmosphere make it an ideal destination for those seeking peace and natural splendor away from crowded tourist spots.

Highlights
  • Stunning views of snow-capped Himalayan peaks
  • Crystal-clear waters of the Lidder River
  • Lush green meadows perfect for picnics
  • Pony rides through scenic trails
  • Photography opportunities at every turn

  • Bird watching in the serene environment
Travel Tips

  • Carry a camera to capture the breathtaking scenery
  • Respect the environment and avoid littering
Aru Valley - Gateway to Kolahoi Glacier

Aru Valley is a picturesque valley located about 12 kilometers from Pahalgam in the Anantnag district. Situated at an altitude of 2,408 meters, it serves as the base camp for treks to the Kolahoi Glacier and the Tarsar-Marsar Lakes. The valley is known for its scenic beauty and tranquil atmosphere.

The valley is surrounded by lush green meadows and snow-capped mountains, with the Aru River flowing through it. The name 'Aru' is believed to have been derived from the local word for a meadow. The valley remains inhabited by Gujjar and Bakarwal tribes who practice transhumance, moving with their livestock to higher pastures in summer.

Aru Valley offers a perfect escape into nature, with opportunities for trekking, camping, and photography. The valley's pristine environment and relatively low tourist footfall make it an ideal destination for those seeking solitude and natural beauty.

Highlights

Yusmarg - The Meadow of Jesus

Yusmarg, which means 'Meadow of Jesus', is a beautiful hill station in the Budgam district of Jammu and Kashmir. According to local legend, Jesus Christ traveled through this valley during his unknown years, which is how it got its name. The valley remains relatively less crowded compared to other tourist destinations in Kashmir.

Situated at an altitude of 2,400 meters, Yusmarg is surrounded by dense forests of pine and fir, with the Doodh Ganga River flowing through it. The valley offers stunning views of the Pir Panjal range, including the peaks of Sunset Peak and Tatakooti Peak.

Yusmarg is an ideal destination for nature lovers and those seeking peace and tranquility. The valley's pristine beauty, with its meadows, forests, and streams, makes it perfect for leisurely walks, picnics, and photography. The area is also home to several nomadic tribes who migrate to higher pastures during summer.

Doodhpathri - The Valley of Milk

Doodhpathri, which translates to 'Valley of Milk', is a beautiful meadow located in the Budgam district of Jammu and Kashmir. The name is derived from the milky white appearance of the water flowing through the valley. According to local legend, the famous saint and poet Sheikh-ul-Alam once prayed here, and milk flowed from the place where he prayed.

Situated at an altitude of 2,730 meters, Doodhpathri is characterized by its rolling green meadows, interspersed with streams and dotted with pine trees. The valley remains covered in snow during winter and transforms into a lush green paradise during summer, with colorful flowers blooming across the meadows.

Doodhpathri is a relatively new addition to Kashmir's tourist circuit and remains less crowded compared to other destinations. Its pristine beauty and tranquil atmosphere make it an ideal destination for nature lovers, photographers, and those seeking peace away from crowded tourist spots.

Kokernag - The Garden of Springs

Kokernag is a famous tourist destination in the Anantnag district of Jammu and Kashmir, known for its gardens, freshwater springs, and trout fishing. The name 'Kokernag' is derived from the Kashmiri words 'Koker' (fowl) and 'Nag' (spring), referring to the spring that resembles a hen's foot in its shape.

The main attraction of Kokernag is its botanical garden, which features a series of freshwater springs. The largest spring, known as the 'Kokernag Spring', has medicinal properties and is believed to cure various ailments. The water from these springs forms a stream that eventually joins the Jhelum River.

Kokernag is also famous for its trout fishing, with the Department of Fisheries maintaining a trout hatchery here. The area is surrounded by lush green forests and offers a peaceful environment for relaxation and recreation. The pleasant climate and natural beauty make Kokernag a popular destination throughout the year.

Highlights

Verinag - Source of the Jhelum River

Verinag is a town in the Anantnag district of Jammu and Kashmir, famous for being the source of the Jhelum River. The main attraction is the Verinag Spring, which emerges from the base of a mountain and forms a circular basin. The spring is named after the Hindu god Varuna, the god of water in Vedic mythology.

The Mughal Emperor Jahangir was so captivated by the beauty of Verinag that he ordered the construction of a garden and a water channel around the spring. His son, Shah Jahan, continued the work and built the existing arcade around the spring. The garden follows the traditional Mughal charbagh pattern, with a central water channel dividing it into four parts.

Verinag Spring is considered one of the largest springs in India and maintains a constant flow throughout the year. The water is crystal clear and has a bluish-green tint, adding to the beauty of the place. The spring is surrounded by lush greenery and offers a peaceful environment for visitors.

Highlights
  • Verinag Spring - source of the Jhelum River
  • Mughal Garden with traditional charbagh pattern
  • Arcade built by Emperor Shah Jahan
  • Crystal clear water with bluish-green tint
  • Ancient temple ruins in the surrounding area
Activities

  • Leisurely walks through the garden

  • Picnics in the garden area
Travel Tips

Aharbal Waterfall - The Niagara of Kashmir

Aharbal Waterfall, often called the 'Niagara of Kashmir', is a stunning waterfall located in the Kulgam district of Jammu and Kashmir. The waterfall is formed by the Vishav River as it plunges from a height of about 25 meters into a deep gorge. The name 'Aharbal' is derived from the Kashmiri words 'Ahar' (mill) and 'Bal' (place), referring to the water mills that were once operated by the force of the waterfall.

The waterfall is surrounded by dense forests and offers breathtaking views, especially during the spring when the snow melts and the water flow is at its peak. The mist created by the falling water creates rainbows on sunny days, adding to the magical atmosphere. A suspension bridge near the waterfall offers a thrilling experience and panoramic views of the cascade.

Aharbal Waterfall is part of a larger area that includes meadows and trekking routes. The destination remains relatively less developed compared to other tourist spots in Kashmir, offering a more authentic and peaceful experience for visitors.

Highlights

Wular Lake - Asia's Largest Freshwater Lake

Wular Lake is one of the largest freshwater lakes in Asia, located in the Bandipora district of Jammu and Kashmir. The lake is fed by the Jhelum River and is an important natural resource for the region. It plays a crucial role in the hydrological cycle of the Kashmir Valley and supports a rich biodiversity.

The lake has great historical and religious significance. It is believed to be the remnant of the prehistoric Satisar Lake that once covered the entire Kashmir Valley. According to Hindu mythology, the lake was created by Lord Shiva. The famous island of Zainalank, located in the lake, is associated with the Hindu goddess Zaina.

Wular Lake is a designated Ramsar site, recognizing its importance as a wetland of international significance. The lake supports a variety of fish species and is an important habitat for migratory birds. However, the lake faces environmental challenges due to siltation, pollution, and encroachment.

Travel Tips

  • Check weather conditions before planning boat rides
Manasbal Lake - The Deepest Lake in Kashmir

Manasbal Lake is the deepest lake in Kashmir, located in the Ganderbal district. The name 'Manasbal' is believed to be derived from the Lake Manasarovar, as the lake is considered its replica. The lake is surrounded by three villages: Jarokbal, Kondabal, and Ganderbal, and is known for its pristine beauty and lotus flowers.

The lake is famous for its lotus flowers that bloom during July and August, covering large areas of the lake with vibrant pink and white blossoms. The Mughal Garden, known as the 'Garden of Depth', is located on the eastern side of the lake and was built by Nur Jahan, the wife of Emperor Jahangir.

Manasbal Lake is an important habitat for aquatic birds and supports a variety of fish species. The lake is less crowded compared to Dal Lake, offering a more peaceful and authentic experience for visitors. The surrounding hills provide a beautiful backdrop to the lake, especially during sunrise and sunset.

Highlights
  • Deepest lake in Kashmir with clear waters
  • Lotus flowers blooming in July and August
  • Mughal Garden on the eastern shore
  • Aquatic bird habitat
  • Scenic views of surrounding hills
Activities

Shankaracharya Hill - The Ancient Temple Hill

Shankaracharya Hill, also known as Takht-e-Suleiman (Throne of Solomon), is a hill located in the Zabarwan Mountain range overlooking Srinagar. The hill is named after Adi Shankaracharya, the great Hindu philosopher who visited Kashmir in the 9th century and established the temple at the summit.

The Shankaracharya Temple, dedicated to Lord Shiva, is located at the top of the hill at an altitude of 1,000 feet above the valley floor. The temple is one of the oldest shrines in Kashmir, with its foundation believed to date back to 200 BC. The current structure was renovated by various rulers over the centuries.

The hill offers panoramic views of Srinagar city, Dal Lake, and the surrounding mountains. The climb to the top involves a series of steps, and the temple is an important pilgrimage site for Hindus. The hill also has strategic importance, with a television tower and communication facilities located there.

Highlights
  • Ancient Shankaracharya Temple at the summit
  • Panoramic views of Srinagar and Dal Lake
  • Historical and religious significance
  • Beautiful sunrise and sunset views
  • Strategic location with communication towers
Activities
  • Climbing the hill to visit the temple

Mughal Gardens - Terraced Paradise

The Mughal Gardens of Kashmir are a series of gardens built by the Mughal emperors in the 16th and 17th centuries. The most famous of these are Shalimar Bagh, Nishat Bagh, and Chashme Shahi, all located in Srinagar. These gardens represent the pinnacle of Mughal garden design, combining Persian influences with local traditions.

The gardens follow the charbagh pattern, which divides the garden into four parts by water channels, representing the four rivers of paradise mentioned in the Quran. They are built in terraces, taking advantage of the natural slope of the land, with water flowing from higher to lower terraces through cascades and fountains.

The Mughal Gardens are not just beautiful landscapes but also engineering marvels, with sophisticated water management systems. They were designed as places of relaxation and entertainment for the royal family, with pavilions, shaded walkways, and fragrant flower beds. Today, they are popular tourist attractions, especially during spring when the flowers are in full bloom.

  • Pari Mahal - the garden of fairies with historical significance

Gurez Valley - The Hidden Gem

Dachigam National Park - Home of the Hangul

Dachigam National Park is a protected area located just 22 kilometers from Srinagar. The park covers an area of 141 square kilometers and ranges in altitude from 1,700 to 4,300 meters. The name 'Dachigam' means 'ten villages', referring to the ten villages that were relocated to create the park during the early 20th century.

The park is best known as the habitat of the Hangul, or Kashmir stag, which is the state animal of Jammu and Kashmir and an endangered species. Dachigam is one of the last refuges for this magnificent animal. The park is also home to other wildlife including Himalayan black bear, leopard, musk deer, and numerous bird species.

Dachigam National Park is divided into two sections: Upper Dachigam and Lower Dachigam, with different vegetation and wildlife in each. The park features diverse landscapes including meadows, waterfalls, and alpine pastures. The Dachigam River flows through the park, adding to its scenic beauty.

Amarnath Cave - The Holy Shrine

The Amarnath Cave is a Hindu shrine located in the Indian union territory of Jammu and Kashmir. It is dedicated to Lord Shiva and is one of the most important pilgrimage sites for Hindus. The cave is situated at an altitude of 3,888 meters and is surrounded by snowy mountains.

The main attraction of the cave is the natural formation of an ice stalagmite that resembles a Shiva Lingam. According to Hindu belief, this lingam waxes and wanes with the phases of the moon. The cave also contains two other ice formations representing Lord Ganesha and Goddess Parvati.

The Amarnath Yatra (pilgrimage) takes place annually during the months of July and August, attracting hundreds of thousands of devotees. The pilgrimage can be undertaken via two routes: the traditional Pahalgam route which takes 3-5 days, or the shorter Baltal route which can be completed in 1-2 days. The journey is challenging due to the high altitude and difficult terrain, but is considered highly spiritually rewarding.

Tarsar Marsar Lakes - The Twin Alpine Jewels

Tarsar and Marsar are twin alpine lakes located in the Anantnag district of Jammu and Kashmir. Situated at an altitude of about 4,000 meters, these lakes are considered among the most beautiful in the Kashmir Valley. The lakes are separated by a mountain range and have distinct characteristics.

Tarsar Lake is almond-shaped and is fed by melting snow from the surrounding mountains. Marsar Lake, on the other hand, is more circular and is connected to Tarsar via an underground stream. According to local belief, the water of Marsar Lake is not fit for human consumption, while Tarsar's water is considered pure.

The trek to Tarsar and Marsar Lakes is considered one of the most beautiful in Kashmir, passing through meadows, forests, and high mountain passes. The lakes remain frozen for most of the year and are accessible only during summer months. The area is also home to various species of Himalayan flora and fauna.

Martand Sun Temple - Ancient Architectural Marvel

The Martand Sun Temple is an ancient Hindu temple dedicated to Surya, the Sun God, located in the Anantnag district of Jammu and Kashmir. The temple was built by King Lalitaditya Muktapida of the Karkota dynasty in the 8th century AD. It is considered one of the most important archaeological sites in India.

The temple complex is built in a unique architectural style that blends Gandharan, Gupta, and Chinese forms. The main temple is surrounded by 84 smaller shrines and is situated in a large courtyard. The temple was constructed with massive limestone blocks and features intricate carvings depicting various Hindu deities.

Though the temple is now in ruins, its grandeur is still evident. The site offers panoramic views of the Kashmir Valley, with the Pir Panjal range forming a beautiful backdrop. The temple was destroyed by Sultan Sikandar Butshikan in the 15th century as part of his campaign against Hindu temples in Kashmir.
